Project: Retractable Scissor Staircase Mechanism
Software Used
PTC Creo Parametric: Used for complex kinematic assembly, linkage modeling, and motion simulation.

Purpose / Problem Solved
In urban architecture and tiny-home design, floor space is a premium. Traditional stairs occupy a permanent footprint regardless of use. This Retractable Staircase solves the problem by utilizing a pantograph (scissor) linkage that allows the entire assembly to collapse into a ceiling or wall cavity when not in use, effectively "reclaiming" the floor space.

Key Technical Actions
Kinematic Linkage Design: Engineered a multi-stage scissor mechanism where every joint is constrained to move in synchronization, ensuring all stair treads remain perfectly horizontal throughout the extension and retraction stroke.

Pin & Pivot Constraints: Applied Pin Joints and Slot Motor simulations to validate that the staircase can support its own weight without binding or over-extending.

Tread Alignment Logic: Modeled the steps with a specific geometry that allows them to stack vertically with minimal clearance, maximizing the compact nature of the retracted state.

Support Frame Integration: Designed a heavy-duty mounting bracket (dark grey L-profiles) with integrated pivot rods to transfer the load from the stairs to the structural building frame.

Materials & Design Decisions
Linkage Arms: High-strength Aluminum or Steel (Blue) for a high strength-to-weight ratio.

Treads: Non-slip composite or metal plating (Dark Grey) with rounded leading edges for safety.

Pivot Pins: Hardened Steel bolts with low-friction bushings to ensure smooth, quiet operation over thousands of cycles.

Project Results
Functional Motion Validation: Successfully simulated the full deployment range, ensuring the bottom step reaches the floor at the correct ergonomic angle for climbing.

Space Optimization: Achieved a retraction ratio of approximately 5:1, reducing a full flight of stairs into a compact overhead compartment.

Mechanical Synchronization: Verified that the parallel linkage maintains tread stability, preventing any "tilting" of the steps regardless of the extension percentage.
